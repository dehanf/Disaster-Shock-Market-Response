\begin{abstract}
Natural disasters inflict severe macroeconomic shocks that ripple through
financial markets, yet the dynamics of such shocks in emerging-economy
exchanges remain understudied. Cyclone Ditwah made landfall in eastern
Sri Lanka on 28~November~2025, causing an estimated USD~4.1~billion in
direct damage (approximately 4\% of GDP) and triggering the Colombo Stock
Exchange's (CSE) worst weekly decline since November~2022. This paper
employs a classical event-study framework on a self-collected dataset of
71,044 daily OHLCV records for 289 CSE-listed stocks spanning
February~2025 to February~2026. After cleaning the dataset and removing outliers, market-model parameters
($\hat{\alpha}$,~$\hat{\beta}$) are estimated for 203 qualifying stocks categorized across 19 CSE sectors. Energy and Insurance
recorded the largest full-window CARs at $-17.93\%$ and $-12.07\%$
respectively, while Transportation Services sustained a significant full-window
loss of $-10.09\%$. Parametric cross-sectional $t$-tests and non-parametric
Wilcoxon signed-rank tests identify four sectors with statistically
significant negative median CARs: Consumer Services ($p < 0.001$), Insurance 
($p=0.004$), Diversified Financials ($p=0.008$), and Utilities ($p=0.027$), while the
market-wide shock remains significant ($p = 0.013 < 0.05$). Recovery analysis
shows that 10 of 19 sectors failed to recover within the 30-trading-day
observation window. Market microstructure analysis reveals a two-phase
response: a brief volatility spike at landfall ($t=-2.40$, $p=0.023$)
followed by a persistent liquidity freeze, with total daily trading volume
falling approximately 46.6\% post-event ($t=6.88$, $p<0.001$).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%%=============================================================================

Sri Lanka's economy, still recovering under an IMF Extended Fund Facility
arrangement following the 2022 sovereign debt crisis~\cite{imf2025},
suffered a major setback when Cyclone Ditwah struck the eastern coastline
on 28~November~2025. The World Bank estimated direct physical damage at
USD~4.1~billion (approximately 4\% of GDP~\cite{worldbank2025}), affecting
58,340~hectares of paddy, damaging 247~km of roads and 35~bridges, and
displacing over 209,000~people across all 25~districts
\cite{who2025,ocha2025}.

Financial markets responded sharply. The CSE All-Share Price Index (ASPI)
fell 3.04\% on the first trading session after landfall, extending to a
weekly loss of 5.35\%~--- the worst since mid-November~2022
\cite{brecorder2025}. Net foreign outflows from the exchange accelerated
during December~2025.

Despite these observable effects, no peer-reviewed study has systematically
quantified the impact of Cyclone Ditwah on individual stocks or market
sectors. This gap matters for three stakeholder groups: investors who need
to price disaster risk into portfolios; policymakers who must design market
stabilisation interventions; and researchers who require empirical evidence
on emerging-market resilience. This paper addresses this gap using a
rigorous event-study framework~\cite{mackinlay1997} applied to a
purpose-built, high-resolution CSE dataset.

This study is organised around five research questions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{RQ1 (Market Shock):} Did Cyclone Ditwah produce a
        statistically significant shock to aggregate CSE returns?
  \item \textbf{RQ2 (Speed of Reaction):} How quickly did the market
        incorporate the disaster shock --- was the impact concentrated on
        landfall day or did it accumulate over subsequent trading sessions?
  \item \textbf{RQ3 (Anticipation):} Was there a statistically significant
        pre-event price reaction consistent with investors pricing in
        disaster risk before official landfall?
  \item \textbf{RQ4 (Sector Heterogeneity):} Which CSE sectors were most
        negatively affected, and does the sector-level evidence differ
        between parametric and non-parametric tests?
  \item \textbf{RQ5 (Recovery):} Which sectors recovered to pre-event
        return levels within the 30-trading-day observation window, and
        which sustained persistent damage?
\end{enumerate}

In addition to these primary questions, the paper examines how the cyclone
altered broader market microstructure --- specifically volatility and
liquidity dynamics in the days surrounding the event.

%%=============================================================================
\section{Literature Review}
%%=============================================================================

The event-study methodology, formalised by MacKinlay~\cite{mackinlay1997},
estimates stock returns absent a discrete event and attributes the residual
to that event. Its application to natural disasters has established several
baseline findings. Shelor et al.~\cite{shelor1992} showed that the 1989
Loma Prieta earthquake produced significant abnormal returns in insurance
stocks, driven by anticipated premium increases --- a pattern this paper
examines in the CSE context. Worthington and Valadkhani~\cite{worthington2004}
extended disaster event studies to a broader set of events and documented
significant but short-lived negative abnormal returns concentrated in sectors
with direct physical exposure.

The bulk of this literature focuses on developed markets. Evidence from
emerging and frontier exchanges is sparser and reveals systematically
different dynamics. Nikkinen and Vahamaa~\cite{nikkinen2010} found that
frontier-market indices sustained negative abnormal returns for several weeks
following the 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami, far longer than developed-market
counterparts, attributing this to thinner liquidity and lower investor
confidence. This motivates the present study's explicit focus on recovery
dynamics and microstructure.

At the sector level, Bourdeau-Brien and Kryzanowski~\cite{bourdeau2017}
documented that transportation and materials sectors recovered more slowly
than the headline index following Canadian flooding events --- a finding
directly relevant to our RQ5 design. The closest analogue to the present
study is Thai~\cite{thai2011}, who found manufacturing CARs of up to
$-15\%$ over a 30-day window following the 2011 Thailand floods in an
emerging-market setting with comparable infrastructure damage.

On market microstructure, Chordia et al.~\cite{chordia2001} established
that liquidity deteriorates precisely during high-uncertainty events,
motivating our use of daily volume as a liquidity proxy and our two-phase
interpretation of the post-landfall response.

ASPI daily OHLC data were collected separately and merged on date to
provide the market benchmark return $R_{m}$ required by the market model.
Sector classifications were derived from CSE sector listings, mapping each
stock to one of 19 industry groups.

\subsection{Data Cleaning}

\textbf{Sector mapping.} 
A manually verified sector mapping was applied to
the working dataset with 276 symbols. 4 stocks which were not part of the sector mapping ('BBH.N0000' 'CALC.U0000' 'CALI.U0000' 'CSF.N0000') were removed from the dataset, leaving 70310 records

\textbf{Removal of Preference shares.} 
The dataset was filtered to retain only ordinary equities (N0000, X0000), excluding preference shares (P0000). This exclusion prevents estimation bias, as the market model's foundational assumption that returns are driven by market-wide and firm-specific factors holds for common equity but breaks down for fixed-income hybrids and pooled assets. This leaves the dataset with 70086 records total.

\textbf{Removal of Outlier Records.} 
Removed 2 records ('COLO.N0000 - 2025-10-08' ,'CIC.X0000 - 2025-10-07') as they were prooven to be outliers

\textbf{Microstructure filtering.} 
 8 penny stocks and 70 consistently illiquid stocks were explicitly excluded from the dataset. Removing penny stocks prevents extreme artificial returns caused by bid-ask bounce at low nominal prices. Similarly, removing low-volume stocks prevents "stale pricing"; where infrequent trading creates zero-return intervals that artificially bias the market-model beta estimates downward and violate the assumption of synchronous market trading. This filtering leaves the dataset with 207 instruments and 52046 records

\textbf{Regression qualification.} The market model was estimated only for
stocks with at least 60 clean trading observations in the estimation window
(Day~$-120$ to Day~$-6$), excluding 4 thinly traded stocks and leaving 203
stocks and 51,779 rows.

%%=============================================================================
\section{Methodology}
%%=============================================================================

We adopt the standard event-study framework of
MacKinlay~\cite{mackinlay1997}. This method estimates the return of each
stock absent the event and compares it with observed returns; the difference
is taken as the abnormal return, i.e., the portion of price movement
attributable directly to Cyclone Ditwah.

\subsection{Event Definition and Windows}

The event date ($t=0$) is 28~November~2025 (cyclone landfall). Two
non-overlapping windows are defined:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Estimation window:} Day~$-120$ to Day~$-6$
        (${\sim}115$ trading days). The gap between Day~$-6$ and Day~$-5$
        isolates the estimation period from potential anticipation effects.
  \item \textbf{Event window:} Day~$-5$ to Day~$+30$ (36 trading days).
        Spans pre-cyclone anticipation, the event shock, and post-disaster
        recovery.
\end{itemize}

All day indices are expressed in trading days, not calendar days, to avoid
bias from weekends and public holidays.

\subsection{Market Model}

Individual stock returns are modelled as a linear function of market
returns over the estimation window:
\begin{equation}
  R_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta_i R_{mt} + \varepsilon_{it}
  \label{eq:market-model}
\end{equation}
where $R_{it}$ is the daily return of stock $i$ on day $t$, $R_{mt}$ is
the ASPI return, $\alpha_i$ is the stock-specific intercept, $\beta_i$ is
the market-sensitivity coefficient, and $\varepsilon_{it}$ is a zero-mean
error term.

\subsection{Abnormal Returns and CARs}

Abnormal returns in the event window are the residual between the actual
return and the counterfactual predicted by the fitted model:
\begin{equation}
  AR_{it} = R_{it} - \bigl(\hat{\alpha}_i + \hat{\beta}_i R_{mt}\bigr)
\end{equation}
Cumulative abnormal returns aggregate over any sub-window $[t_1, t_2]$:
\begin{equation}
  CAR_i(t_1, t_2) = \sum_{t=t_1}^{t_2} AR_{it}
\end{equation}
We report CARs for four windows: pre-event $[-5,-1]$, landfall day $[0,0]$,
immediate aftermath $[0,+5]$, and the complete event window $[-5,+30]$.
Sector-level CARs are the cross-sectional mean of individual stock CARs
within each sector.

\subsection{Statistical Testing}

\paragraph{Parametric cross-sectional $t$-test.}
For each window $[t_1,t_2]$, the $CAR_i$ values across all stocks (or
stocks within a sector) are tested against a null mean of zero:
\begin{equation}
  t = \frac{\overline{CAR}}{s / \sqrt{N}}
\end{equation}
where $\overline{CAR}$ is the cross-sectional mean, $s$ is the standard
deviation, and $N$ is the number of stocks (two-tail , $\alpha=0.05$).

\paragraph{Non-parametric Wilcoxon signed-rank test.}
Because CSE return distributions exhibit fat tails and skewness typical of
frontier markets, we apply the Wilcoxon signed-rank test as a robust check
on sector-level median CARs. Concordance between parametric and
non-parametric results strengthens confidence; divergence signals that a
sector mean is driven by a small number of extreme stocks rather than a
uniform industry-wide repricing.

\paragraph{Recovery analysis.}
For each sector, we identify the trough CAR within the event window and the
earliest subsequent day on which the cumulative mean CAR returns to zero
(the recovery day). Sectors that do not cross zero before Day~$+30$ are
classified as unrecovered.

\paragraph{Microstructure $t$-tests.}
Daily market volatility is proxied by the average High-Low price spread
across all qualifying stocks; market liquidity is measured by total daily
trading volume. Welch's independent-samples $t$-test (unequal variances)
compares daily means across symmetric 25-day pre-event (Day~$-30$ to
Day~$-6$) and post-event (Day~0 to Day~$+24$) windows.

\subsection{RQ1: Market-Wide Shock}
%%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Figure~\ref{fig:aspi} plots the ASPI daily close from February~2025 to
March~2026. The index peaked near 23,600 in mid-November~2025, then fell
from 22,844 (26~November) to approximately 21,431 on 5~December ; a
peak-to-trough drop of approximately 6.2\% over six trading days. The index
subsequently recovered above 23,000 by January~2026.

%%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{RQ2: Speed of Market Reaction}
%%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The landfall-day CAR$(0,0)$ of $-0.13\%$ is economically negligible and
statistically insignificant ($p = 0.4457 >> 0.05$). The largest single-window loss of $-2.04\%$
accumulated over the immediate aftermath window (Days~0 to~$+5$), while
the full-window loss of $-2.57\%$ confirms that the shock continued
accumulating through Day~$+30$. This pattern of gradual repricing, with
virtually no single-day spike is consistent with semi-strong market
efficiency~\cite{mackinlay1997}: the market did not absorb the shock
instantaneously, but incorporated damage information progressively as
assessments were published (RQ2: \textbf{gradual, not immediate}).

%%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{RQ3: Pre-Event Anticipation Effects}
%%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The pre-event market-wide CAR$(-5,-1)$ of $-0.56\%$ (Table~\ref{tab:market_car})
indicates that abnormal losses were already accumulating before landfall.
At the sector level, Table~\ref{tab:car} shows Energy with the
largest pre-event loss (CAR$(-5,-1) = -6.19\%$) and Transportation
 second ($-5.57\%$), consistent with investors pricing in disaster
risk as official cyclone warnings intensified during the five trading days
before landfall. The concurrent volume decline beginning approximately ten
trading days before landfall (Figure~\ref{fig:liq_ts}) corroborates this:
liquidity withdrawal preceded the physical event (RQ3: \textbf{yes, in
physically exposed sectors}).

\textbf{Parametric--non-parametric divergence.}
The Wilcoxon test identifies four sectors with significant median CARs: Consumer Services ($W=3$, $p<0.001$), Insurance ($W=0$, $p=0.004$), Diversified Financials ($W=97$, $p=0.008$), and Utilities ($W=4$, $p=0.027$). Transportation

between the mean-based and median-based tests illustrates the value of
reporting both: the $t$-test detects individual outlier shocks; the
Wilcoxon test identifies sectors where the repricing was broad-based
(RQ4: \textbf{substantial heterogeneity; four sectors with broad-based
significant negative returns}).

10 of 19 sectors did not recover to their pre-event CAR baseline within the 30-trading-day window. The most persistent losses were in Real Estate (trough $-12.32\%$ at Day~21), Energy ($-12.15\%$ at Day~15), Transportation ($-11.56\%$ at Day~19), and Insurance ($-11.45\%$ at Day~30). Consumer Services also failed to recover, reaching its trough of $-11.28\%$ at Day~30.

Notably, the ASPI index returned above 23,000 by January~2026
(Figure~\ref{fig:aspi}), suggesting broad market recovery within
approximately 40~trading days; yet sector-level CARs for ten sectors
remained substantially negative at Day~$+30$. This divergence between
index-level and sector-level recovery confirms that headline index recovery
can mask persistent sector-specific damage and should not be used as the
sole gauge of disaster impact (RQ5: \textbf{10 of 19 sectors unrecovered;
index recovery masks sector-level persistence}).

\textbf{Volatility: a delayed spike followed by sustained compression.}
The aggregate post-event mean spread (2.61~LKR) is significantly higher
than the pre-event mean (2.21~LKR; $t=-2.40$, $p=0.023 < 0.05$). However,
Figure~\ref{fig:vol_ts} reveals a two-phase pattern invisible in the
aggregate statistic: a sharp intraday uncertainty spike in the immediate
aftermath (peaking near 5.0~LKR around Day~$+10$), followed by progressive
compression below the pre-event baseline by Day~$+25$.

\textbf{Liquidity: pre-event deterioration and a persistent post-event freeze.}
Figure~\ref{fig:liq_ts} shows volume declining from approximately
Day~$-10$, reaching a minimum on Day~0, briefly rebounding on Days~$+1$
to~$+4$, then remaining well below pre-event levels through Day~$+30$.
The aggregate decline of approximately 46.6\% is highly significant
($t=6.88$, $p < 0.001$). The pre-event deterioration is consistent with
anticipation effects documented in RQ3: investors began reducing market
participation as cyclone warnings intensified.

\textbf{Two-phase interpretation.} These patterns together describe a
characteristic two-phase microstructure response. In Phase~1 (Day~$-10$
to Day~$+1$), anticipation and landfall drove simultaneous liquidity
withdrawal and an acute intraday volatility spike. In Phase~2 (Day~$+2$
onward), the market entered a liquidity freeze: volume remained depressed
and intraday price ranges compressed as uncertainty suppressed both demand
and supply of shares. This pattern contrasts with a panic-selling regime,
which would exhibit elevated volume \emph{and} sustained volatility. The
policy implication is phase-specific: circuit breakers are most relevant
at the acute spike, while liquidity-restoration measures (market-maker
incentives, temporary transaction-cost reductions) are needed to address
the extended freeze.

%%=============================================================================
\section{Discussion}
%%=============================================================================

\paragraph{RQ1 --- Market Shock.}
The full-window market mean CAR of $-2.57\%$ establishes a meaningful
aggregate shock ($t=-2.52$, $p = 0.013 <0.05$), broadly consistent with the
event-study literature on emerging-market natural disasters. Bourdeau-Brien
and Kryzanowski~\cite{bourdeau2017} and Thai~\cite{thai2011} document
comparable magnitudes for similar events in frontier and emerging markets.

\paragraph{RQ2 --- Speed of Reaction.}
The near-zero landfall-day CAR of $-0.13\%$ and the gradual accumulation
of losses across the 30-day window are consistent with semi-strong market
efficiency~\cite{fama1969,mackinlay1997}: prices incorporate successive
damage assessments as they are released, rather than jumping discretely on
Day~0. This stands in contrast to the Shelor et al.~\cite{shelor1992}
finding of a sharp Day~0 insurance spike in the deeper US market, suggesting
that information dissemination is slower in the CSE's frontier setting.

\paragraph{RQ3 --- Anticipation.}
Pre-event losses in Energy and Insurance Sectors, coinciding with
volume deterioration from Day~$-10$, are consistent with the Cutler et
al.~\cite{cutler1989} framework for events with predictable onset. The
anticipation pattern confirms that at least some CSE market participants
act on meteorological forecasts, a useful signal for disaster risk pricing.

\paragraph{RQ4 --- Sector Heterogeneity.}
The parametric--non-parametric divergence for Transportation (large negative
mean, insignificant Wilcoxon) illustrates a finding with direct practical
implications: a sector's mean loss may be dominated by one or two extreme
stocks even where the median stock is unaffected. Investors and
policymakers should therefore assess \emph{both} the scale of the worst-hit
firms \emph{and} the breadth of the industry-wide repricing before
inferring systemic sector exposure. Insurance, Utilities, Consumer Services, and Diversified Financials show concordant significance on both tests, providing stronger evidence of genuine sector-wide repricing than Transportation alone.

\paragraph{RQ5 --- Recovery.}
The finding that 10 of 19 sectors failed to recover within 30~trading days
extends the disaster-finance literature by providing granular sector-level
evidence from a frontier exchange. The ASPI-level recovery within 40~days
mirrors the Nikkinen and Vahamaa~\cite{nikkinen2010} finding of faster
index-level than sector-level recovery in frontier markets following the
2004 tsunami. Sector-disaggregated monitoring is therefore necessary to
identify residual disaster exposure that is masked by headline index
movements.

%%=============================================================================
\section{Limitations and Future Work}
%%=============================================================================

\paragraph{Single-factor market model.}
The market model regresses individual stock returns on the ASPI alone. A
Fama-French three-factor specification would provide a richer counterfactual
and may reduce omitted-variable bias, but factor data for the CSE are not
publicly available at daily frequency, limiting its current feasibility.

\paragraph{Small-sector reliability.}
Four sectors contain a single qualifying stock (Automobiles, Software \&
Services, Household Products, Energy) and two sectors contain only two
stocks (Transportation, Telecommunication Services). Point estimates are
reported for completeness but carry very wide confidence intervals.

\paragraph{Confounding events.}
The estimation window spans a period of ongoing IMF programme negotiations
and macroeconomic uncertainty. Firm-specific confounders during the event
window cannot be individually controlled without a comprehensive corporate
actions database.

\paragraph{Generalisation.}
This study examines a single disaster event on a single frontier exchange.
Cross-event and cross-market replication studies would establish whether the
liquidity-freeze pattern and sector heterogeneity documented here are robust
features of emerging-market disaster responses.

%%=============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
%%=============================================================================

This paper provides the first systematic event-study quantification of
Cyclone Ditwah's impact on the Colombo Stock Exchange. Addressing five
research questions, we establish: (RQ1)~a highly significant market-wide
shock ($-2.57\%$, $p=0.013$); (RQ2)~gradual price discovery consistent
with semi-strong efficiency; (RQ3)~statistically detectable pre-event
anticipation in physically exposed sectors; (RQ4)~substantial sector
heterogeneity, with four sectors showing broad-based significant repricing
(Consumer Services, Insurance, Diversified Financials, Utilities) and
Transportation's large mean loss driven by individual outliers; and
(RQ5)~persistent unrecovered losses in 10 of 19 sectors at Day~$+30$,
despite index-level recovery within approximately 40~trading days.

These results are relevant to investors pricing disaster risk in frontier
exchanges, policymakers designing phase-specific stabilisation interventions,
and researchers studying the limits of market efficiency under extreme
informational shocks.
