\begin{abstract}
Natural disasters inflict severe macroeconomic shocks that ripple through
financial markets, yet the dynamics of such shocks in emerging-economy
exchanges remain understudied. Cyclone Ditwah made landfall in eastern Sri
Lanka on 28~November~2025, causing an estimated USD~4.1~billion in direct
damage (approximately 4\% of GDP) and triggering the Colombo Stock
Exchange's (CSE) worst weekly decline since November~2022. This paper
employs a classical event-study framework on a self-collected dataset of
70,413 daily OHLCV records for 268 CSE-listed stocks spanning
February~2025 to February~2026. Market-model parameters ($\hat{\alpha}$,
$\hat{\beta}$) are estimated for 237 qualifying stocks. Sector-level
cumulative abnormal returns (CARs) are computed across 20 standard CSE
sectors. Transportation and Real Estate recorded the largest full-window
CARs at $-10.03\%$ and $-9.23\%$ respectively, while Commercial Services
showed resilience with a near-zero full-window CAR of $+0.27\%$.
Parametric cross-sectional $t$-tests and non-parametric Wilcoxon
signed-rank tests identify three sectors with statistically significant
negative median CARs: Insurance ($p = 0.009$), Real Estate ($p = 0.017$),
and Diversified Financials ($p = 0.034$), while the market-wide shock is
highly significant ($p < 0.001$). An analysis of market microstructure
reveals a two-phase response: a sharp intraday volatility spike at landfall
($t = 1.84$, $p = 0.076$) followed by a persistent liquidity freeze, with
total daily trading volume falling approximately 35\% post-event
($t = 3.76$, $p < 0.001$).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%%=============================================================================

Sri Lanka's economy, still recovering under an IMF Extended Fund Facility
arrangement following the 2022 sovereign debt crisis \cite{imf2025},
suffered a major setback when Cyclone Ditwah struck the eastern coastline
on 28~November~2025. The World Bank estimated direct physical damage at
USD~4.1~billion (approximately 4\% of GDP \cite{worldbank2025}) affecting
58,340~hectares of paddy, damaging 247~km of roads and 35~bridges, and
displacing over 209,000~people across all 25~districts
\cite{who2025, ocha2025}.

Financial markets responded sharply. The CSE All-Share Price Index (ASPI)
fell 3.04\% on the first trading session after landfall, extending to a
weekly loss of 5.35\%—the worst since mid-November~2022
\cite{brecorder2025}. Net foreign outflows from the exchange accelerated
during December~2025.

Despite these observable effects, no peer-reviewed study has systematically
quantified the impact of Cyclone Ditwah on individual stocks or market
sectors. This gap matters for three stakeholder groups: investors who need
to price disaster risk into portfolios; policymakers who must design market
stabilisation interventions; and researchers who require empirical evidence
on emerging-market resilience. This paper addresses this gap using a
rigorous event-study framework \cite{mackinlay1997} applied to a
purpose-built, high-resolution CSE dataset.

This study reports on three research questions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{RQ1:} Did Cyclone Ditwah affect returns in the Colombo
        Stock Exchange?
  \item \textbf{RQ2:} How quickly did the market react to the cyclone?
  \item \textbf{RQ3:} Was there any predictive price reaction before the
        event?
\end{enumerate}

In addition to these primary research questions, the paper examines which
CSE sectors were most negatively affected, which were resilient or
positively impacted, whether sectors reacted at different speeds, and how
the cyclone altered broader market microstructure.

\subsection{Primary Dataset}

Daily OHLCV data for all CSE-listed stocks were collected via a custom
Python web-scraping pipeline querying the exchange's public data interface.
The raw dataset comprised 71,044 rows. After cleaning (detailed in
Section~2.2), the working dataset contains 70,029 rows.

ASPI daily OHLC data were collected separately and merged on date to
provide the market benchmark return $R_{m}$ required by the market model.
Sector classifications were derived from CSE sector listings, mapping each
stock to one of 20 sectors.

\subsection{Data Cleaning}

\textbf{Suffix handling.} CSE tickers carry suffixes encoding share class.
Records with suffixes U0000 (units, $n=407$) and P0000 (preference shares,
$n=224$) were removed. Three symbols were subsequently removed explicitly:
Browns Beach Hotels (BBH) and Nation Lanka Finance (CSF) due to delisting
and data quality issues, and INME which held no observations within the
estimation window (Day~$-120$ to Day~$-6$). This yielded 70,029~rows
across voting (N0000, 65,279~rows) and non-voting (X0000, 4,807~rows)
shares.

\textbf{Sector mapping.} A manually verified sector mapping was applied to
the working dataset with 263 symbols.

\textbf{Regression qualification.} The market model was estimated only for
stocks with at least 60 clean trading observations in the estimation window
(Day~$-120$ to Day~$-6$), excluding 6 thinly traded stocks and leaving 257
stocks. Stocks with $\hat{\beta} \notin [-3,\,5]$ were subsequently removed
as outliers (20~stocks dropped), yielding a final sample of 237~stocks for
downstream analysis.

%%=============================================================================
\section{Methodology}
%%=============================================================================

We adopt the standard event-study framework of
MacKinlay~\cite{mackinlay1997}. This method estimates the return of each
stock absent the event and compares it with observed returns; the difference
is taken as the abnormal return, i.e.\ the portion of price movement
attributable directly to Cyclone Ditwah.

\subsection{Event Definition and Windows}

The event date ($t = 0$) is 28~November~2025 (cyclone landfall). Two
non-overlapping windows are defined:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Estimation window:} Day~$-120$ to Day~$-6$
        (${\sim}115$ trading days). The gap between Day~$-6$ and Day~$-5$
        isolates the estimation period from potential anticipation effects.
  \item \textbf{Event window:} Day~$-5$ to Day~$+30$ (36 trading days).
        Spans pre-cyclone anticipation, the event shock, and post-disaster
        recovery.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Market Model}

Individual stock returns are modelled as a linear function of market
returns over the estimation window:
\begin{equation}
  R_{it} = \alpha_i + \beta_i R_{mt} + \varepsilon_{it}
  \label{eq:market-model}
\end{equation}
where $R_{it}$ is the daily return of stock $i$ on day $t$, $R_{mt}$ is
the ASPI return, $\alpha_i$ is the stock-specific intercept, $\beta_i$ is
the market-sensitivity coefficient, and $\varepsilon_{it}$ is a zero-mean
error term.

\subsection{Abnormal Returns}

Abnormal returns in the event window are the residual between the actual
return and the counterfactual predicted by the fitted model:
\begin{equation}
  AR_{it} = R_{it} - \bigl(\hat{\alpha}_i + \hat{\beta}_i R_{mt}\bigr)
  \label{eq:ar}
\end{equation}
Cumulative abnormal returns aggregate over any sub-window $[t_1, t_2]$:
\begin{equation}
  CAR_i(t_1, t_2) = \sum_{t=t_1}^{t_2} AR_{it}
  \label{eq:car}
\end{equation}
We report CARs for four windows: pre-event $[-5,-1]$, landfall day
$[0,0]$, immediate aftermath $[0,+5]$, and the complete event window
$[-5,+30]$.

\subsection{Statistical Testing}
\label{sec:stat_testing}

\paragraph{Parametric cross-sectional $t$-test.}
To determine whether observed abnormal returns are statistically
distinguishable from zero, cross-sectional $t$-tests were applied at both
the market-wide and sector levels. For each window $[t_1,t_2]$, the
$CAR_i$ values across all stocks (or stocks within a sector) are treated as
a sample and tested against a null mean of zero:
\begin{equation}
  t = \frac{\overline{CAR}}{s / \sqrt{N}}
  \label{eq:tstat}
\end{equation}
where $\overline{CAR}$ is the cross-sectional mean, $s$ is the
cross-sectional standard deviation, and $N$ is the number of stocks. This
is a one-sample $t$-test against a population mean of zero (two-sided,
$\alpha = 0.05$).

\paragraph{Non-parametric Wilcoxon signed-rank test.}
Because financial return distributions in thin, emerging-market exchanges
routinely exhibit fat tails, skewness, and extreme outliers, relying
exclusively on the parametric $t$-test risks inflated Type~I error rates.
We therefore apply the Wilcoxon signed-rank test as a robust secondary
check on the full-window sector-level CAR distributions. The Wilcoxon test
evaluates whether the \emph{median} abnormal return is significantly
different from zero without assuming normality, making it insensitive to
the influence of a small number of extreme-performing equities. For each
sector with $N > 1$ stocks, the test statistic $W$ is computed from the
signed ranks of the $CAR_i$ values. Concordance between parametric and
non-parametric results strengthens confidence in the findings; divergence
signals that the sector's mean is driven by outliers rather than a uniform
industry-wide repricing.

\paragraph{Volatility and liquidity $t$-tests.}
To assess disaster-induced shifts in market microstructure, daily market
volatility was proxied using the High-Low price spread
($\text{Spread}_{t} = \text{High}_{t} - \text{Low}_{t}$), and market
liquidity was measured via total daily trading volume. Daily averages of
these metrics were computed across all qualifying stocks for two symmetric
25-day windows: a pre-event window (Day~$-30$ to Day~$-6$) and a
post-landfall window (Day~0 to Day~$+24$). Welch's independent-samples
$t$-test (unequal variances assumed) was used to compare the daily average
spread and daily average volume between the two windows.

\subsection{ASPI Index Trajectory}

Figure~\ref{fig:aspi} plots the ASPI daily close from February~2025 to
March~2026. The index opened at approximately 16,700 in February~2025,
dipped to a local trough near 15,000 in late April~2025, then recovered
steadily to a pre-cyclone peak of approximately 23,600 in
mid-November~2025. Following landfall on 28~November~2025, the index
declined sharply from 22,844 (26~November) to a local trough of
approximately 21,431 on 5~December~2025—a peak-to-trough drop of
approximately 6.2\% over six trading days. The index subsequently
recovered, returning above 23,000 by January~2026 and reaching
approximately 24,000 by February~2026, suggesting the market largely
absorbed the cyclone shock within approximately 40 trading days.

\textbf{Most affected sectors.}
Transportation recorded the largest full-window CAR at $-10.03\%$, with
its trajectory reaching a trough of approximately $-12\%$ around Day~$+26$
before a partial recovery. Real Estate followed at $-9.23\%$, consistent
with the cyclone's documented damage to 247~km of roads, 35~bridges, and
coastal property. Insurance recorded the steepest immediate aftermath
CAR$(0,+5)$ at $-4.71\%$, reflecting anticipated claims exposure.
Software \& Services ($-7.41\%$) and Utilities ($-6.69\%$) also sustained
substantial full-window losses, likely reflecting infrastructure disruption
and power outage effects.

\textbf{Anticipation effects.}
In the pre-event window, Transportation shows a CAR$(-5,-1)$ of $-4.53\%$
and Telecommunication Services $-3.53\%$—the two largest pre-event
negative CARs across all 20~sectors—confirming that abnormal losses were
accumulating before official landfall as investors reacted to cyclone
warnings.

\textbf{Resilient sectors.}
Materials remained near zero throughout ($-1.75\%$). Commercial Services
($+0.27\%$) and Automobiles ($+2.43\%$) posted near-zero or positive
full-window CARs, possibly reflecting reconstruction-driven demand. On
landfall day alone, Retailing recorded an anomalous $+2.25\%$, the only
sector with a substantial positive single-day abnormal return, possibly
reflecting panic-buying ahead of the storm.

\textbf{Parametric results.}
Cross-sectional $t$-tests confirm a highly significant market-wide shock
($t = -6.14$, $p < 0.001$, $N = 237$). At the sector level, only Real
Estate achieves significance at the 5\% level ($t = -2.55$, $p = 0.021$,
$N = 17$). Transportation, despite the largest mean CAR of $-10.03\%$,
does not reach significance ($t = -1.16$, $p = 0.366$, $N = 3$),
reflecting both the small sample and the tight, near-zero box visible in
Figure~\ref{fig:car_boxplot} that confirms the mean is outlier-driven.
Sectors such as Diversified Financials and Consumer Services also show
non-significant results despite moderate negative means, driven by large
cross-sectional dispersion among their many constituent stocks.

\textbf{Non-parametric corroboration.}
The Wilcoxon signed-rank test reveals three sectors with statistically
significant median CARs at the 5\% level. Insurance is the most
significant ($W = 7$, $p = 0.009$), with a median CAR of $-8.57\%$,
reflecting broad-based claims exposure across all 12 constituent stocks.
Real Estate ($W = 27$, $p = 0.017$, median $-5.43\%$) and Diversified
Financials ($W = 211$, $p = 0.034$, median $-5.79\%$) are also
significant. Notably, Diversified Financials passes the Wilcoxon test
despite a non-significant $t$-test ($p = 0.420$), indicating that its
mean CAR is suppressed by a large positive outlier (visible in
Figure~\ref{fig:car_boxplot}) while the majority of its 38 stocks
experienced genuine negative abnormal returns. For Transportation, the
Wilcoxon test fails to reject the null ($W = 1$, $p = 0.500$),
consistent with the mean being outlier-driven. The divergence between
the $t$-test and Wilcoxon results for Diversified Financials is
precisely the kind of finding the non-parametric test is designed to
surface: when a sector's mean is distorted by a single extreme equity,
the Wilcoxon median test provides a more reliable picture of the
typical stock-level experience.

\end{figure}

\textbf{Volatility: a Day-0 spike followed by sustained compression.}
Figure~\ref{fig:vol_ts} reveals a pattern the aggregate $t$-test statistic
alone does not capture. The High-Low spread reaches approximately
17.8~LKR on Day~0—the highest value in the entire Day~$-30$ to Day~$+30$
window—indicating a sharp burst of intraday price uncertainty at the moment
of landfall. This spike is immediately followed by a sustained downward
trend: by Day~$+10$ the spread has fallen below 11~LKR, and by Day~$+25$
it stabilises near 5--6~LKR, well below the pre-event baseline. As a
result, the post-event window aggregate mean (8.87~LKR) is actually
\emph{lower} than the pre-event mean (10.77~LKR), and the Welch $t$-test
of this aggregate difference is not significant at the 5\% level
($t = 1.84$, $p = 0.076$). The correct interpretation is a two-phase volatility
response: an acute landfall-day uncertainty spike, followed by progressive
compression as thinning liquidity reduced the range of transactions
executed each day.

\textbf{Liquidity: pre-event deterioration and a persistent post-event freeze.}
Figure~\ref{fig:liq_ts} reveals that the volume decline began approximately
10 trading days before landfall—consistent with the anticipation effects
observed in sector-level CARs—and reached a window minimum of approximately
60 million shares on Day~0. This pre-landfall deterioration is itself
informative: investors began reducing market participation as cyclone
warnings intensified, creating a liquidity drought that preceded the
physical disaster. Volume briefly rebounded on Days~$+2$ to~$+4$ (to
approximately 225 million shares), likely reflecting opportunistic or
rebalancing trades, before collapsing again and remaining well below
pre-event levels through Day~$+30$. The aggregate decline of approximately
35\% is highly significant ($t = 3.76$, $p < 0.001$).

\textbf{Interpretation: a two-phase microstructure response.}
Taken together, the figures point to a two-phase response. In the first
phase (approximately Day~$-10$ to Day~$+1$), anticipation and landfall
drove simultaneous liquidity withdrawal and a brief but sharp intraday
volatility spike. In the second phase (Day~$+2$ onward), the market
entered a \emph{liquidity freeze}: volume remained severely depressed and
intraday price ranges compressed as uncertainty suppressed both demand and
supply of shares. This pattern contrasts with a panic-selling regime, which
would exhibit elevated volume \emph{and} sustained volatility. The policy
implication is phase-specific: circuit breakers are most relevant at the
Day~0 spike, while liquidity-restoration measures—market-maker incentives,
temporary transaction cost reductions—are needed to address the
longer-duration freeze.

%%=============================================================================
\section{Discussion}
%%=============================================================================

\textbf{Market-wide shock.}
The full-window market mean CAR of $-4.42\%$ across 237~qualifying stocks,
confirmed by a highly significant $t$-statistic ($t = -6.14$, $p < 0.001$),
establishes that Cyclone Ditwah induced a meaningful aggregate shock on the
CSE. The near-zero landfall-day CAR$(0,0)$ of $-0.03\%$ is striking: the
single-day impact was negligible market-wide, and the bulk of losses
accumulated over subsequent days as damage assessments emerged. This
pattern of gradual repricing is consistent with the semi-strong form of the
Efficient Market Hypothesis, in which successively released public
information is incorporated incrementally into prices.

\textbf{Anticipation effects.}
Transportation's pre-event CAR$(-5,-1)$ of $-4.53\%$ and Telecom Services'
$-3.53\%$ are the two largest pre-event negative CARs across all
20~sectors, suggesting investors began pricing in disaster risk as cyclone
warnings intensified before official landfall. These anticipation patterns
are consistent with semi-strong form market efficiency
\cite{mackinlay1997}. Notably, the volume time series
(Figure~\ref{fig:liq_ts}) corroborates this: liquidity began deteriorating
from approximately Day~$-10$, simultaneous with the onset of pre-event
abnormal returns in vulnerable sectors.

\textbf{Sector heterogeneity and parametric/non-parametric divergence.}
Transportation ($-10.03\%$) and Real Estate ($-9.23\%$) bore the greatest
cumulative mean losses, consistent with the cyclone's documented damage to
247~km of roads, 35~bridges, and coastal property \cite{who2025, ocha2025}.
However, the Wilcoxon test identifies three sectors with statistically
significant median repricing: Insurance ($p = 0.009$), Real Estate
($p = 0.017$), and Diversified Financials ($p = 0.034$). Transportation,
despite its large negative mean, fails both tests—its box in
Figure~\ref{fig:car_boxplot} is tight and centred near zero, confirming
the mean is outlier-driven across only three stocks. The most instructive
divergence is Diversified Financials: its $t$-test is non-significant
($p = 0.420$) because a single large positive outlier inflates the mean,
yet the Wilcoxon test reveals the median stock in the sector did experience
significant negative abnormal returns ($p = 0.034$). Insurance shows the
opposite pattern—a non-significant $t$-test ($p = 0.257$) but the
strongest Wilcoxon result ($p = 0.009$), driven by consistently negative
returns across its 12 stocks rather than a large mean. These divergences
illustrate why the non-parametric test is essential in thin, heterogeneous
markets: the mean CAR is an unreliable guide to whether a typical investor
in a sector was affected.

Insurance recorded the steepest immediate aftermath CAR$(0,+5)$ at
$-4.71\%$, reflecting anticipated claims exposure. Software \& Services
($-7.41\%$) and Utilities ($-6.69\%$) also sustained substantial losses,
likely reflecting infrastructure disruption. Materials ($-1.75\%$) remained
near zero throughout, and Commercial Services ($+0.27\%$) and Automobiles
($+2.43\%$) posted positive full-window CARs, possibly reflecting
reconstruction-driven demand.

\textbf{Index versus sector recovery divergence.}
The ASPI returned above 23,000 by January~2026, suggesting broad market
recovery within approximately 40~trading days. However, sector-level CARs
at Day~$+30$ remain substantially negative for Transportation, Real Estate,
and Software \& Services, indicating that index-level recovery masked
persistent sector-specific damage. Headline index recovery does not imply
uniform stock-level recovery, and sector-disaggregated monitoring is
necessary to capture residual disaster exposure.

\textbf{Market microstructure and the two-phase response.}
The microstructure evidence enriches the asset pricing results with a
temporal dimension. The High-Low spread time series reveals a sharp
intraday volatility spike on Day~0 (${\sim}17.8$~LKR, the window
maximum), immediately followed by a sustained compression to well below
pre-event levels. Simultaneously, the volume time series shows that the
liquidity deterioration began approximately 10 days before landfall, hit
its minimum at Day~0, and never recovered within the observation window.
Together these patterns describe a two-phase response: a brief acute shock
at landfall followed by a persistent liquidity freeze. The policy
implication is phase-specific: circuit breakers are most relevant at the
Day~0 spike, whereas longer-duration liquidity-restoration tools are
required to address the extended freeze that follows.

%%=============================================================================
\section{Limitations and Future Work}
%%=============================================================================

\paragraph{Single-factor market model.}
The market model regresses individual stock returns on the ASPI alone. A
multi-factor specification incorporating size, value, and momentum factors
(as in the Fama-French framework) would provide a richer counterfactual and
may reduce omitted-variable bias. Future work will also incorporate
macroeconomic control variables from the Central Bank of Sri Lanka and IMF
country reports \cite{imf2025}.

\paragraph{Volatility proxy.}
The High-Low daily price spread is a widely used but imperfect proxy for
realised volatility. While it successfully captured the Day~0 intraday
uncertainty spike (${\sim}17.8$~LKR) and subsequent compression, it does
not account for intraday price paths, bid-ask bounce, or overnight gaps.
Access to tick-by-tick data would permit the use of realised variance
estimators and would better decompose the Day~0 spike into its fundamental
versus noise components.

\paragraph{Small-sector reliability.}
Five sectors contain fewer than five stocks (Energy $N=2$, Automobiles
$N=1$, Software \& Services $N=1$, Telecommunication Services $N=2$, Food
Retailing $N=3$). Point estimates are reported for completeness but carry
very wide confidence intervals; their statistical tests are either
underpowered or not computable. Future work should aggregate these into
broader categories or expand coverage to OTC-traded instruments.

\paragraph{Sample period and confounding events.}
The estimation window spans a period of ongoing IMF programme negotiations
and macroeconomic uncertainty \cite{imf2025}. Firm-specific confounders
during the event window—earnings releases, rights issues, management
changes—cannot be individually controlled without a comprehensive corporate
actions database, which future CSE event studies should incorporate.

\paragraph{Generalisation.}
This study examines a single disaster event on a single frontier exchange.
Caution is warranted in generalising to other disaster types or markets.
Cross-event and cross-market replication studies would establish whether the
liquidity-freeze pattern and sector heterogeneity documented here are robust
features of emerging-market disaster responses.
